##Introduction
Grammatical Type: noun masc.

Occurrences: 2x HB (2/0/0); 0x Sir; 0x Qum; 0x inscr. (Total:
2).


* Torah: Lev 11:13; Deut 14:12.


##1. Root and Comparative Material

<b>A.1</b>  <i>Root</i>: Many Hebrew dictionaries and commentaries derive the
noun  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> from the root  <span dir="rtl">פרס</span>, meaning ‘to break’ or ‘to tear’.<sup id="fnref:2"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:2')">2</a></sup>
In two instances (Mic 3:3; Lam 4:4), some scholars propose that the
verbal root  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַשׂ</span> represents a variant spelling of  <span dir="rtl">פרס</span>.<sup id="fnref:3"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:3')">3</a></sup>
However, this hypothesis is not widely accepted, and some
dictionaries do not seem to adopt the assumption of such textual
corruption by not mentioning these two texts in the lemma
 <span dir="rtl">פָּרַסָּ</span>.<sup id="fnref:4"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:4')">4</a></sup> In the case of Lamentations 4:4, it is also plausible
that that the verb form derives instead from the root meaning ‘to
spread out’, a sense attested elsewhere in Lamentations (1:10,
13).<sup id="fnref:5"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:5')">5</a></sup> Despite the uncertainties surrounding these two texts, they
will be included in the study for the sake of completeness. In
addition to the examples from the Hebrew Bible, there is also a
Qumran text in which Clines interprets  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַשׂ</span> in a similar
manner.<sup id="fnref:6"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:6')">6</a></sup>

The root appears in various verbal forms across a range of biblical and
Qumran texts.

* <i>Qal</i>  <span dir="rtl">פָרֹס</span> in Isaiah 58:7 conveys the meaning ‘to break (bread)’.
* <i>Qal</i>  <span dir="rtl">יׅפְרְסוּ וְלאֹ</span> in Jeremiah 16:7 is understood as ‘and they
will not break [bread] for them in the morning’.
* <i>Qal</i>  <span dir="rtl">וּפָרְשׂוּ</span> in Micah 3:3 is interpreted as ‘to chop into pieces
(bones)’.
* <i>Qal</i>  <span dir="rtl">פֹּרֵשׂ</span> in Lamentations 4:4 carries the meaning ‘(not) to
break (bread)’.
* <i>Qal</i>  <span dir="rtl">פרשנו</span> in 4QMMT C line 7 occurs in the (reconstructed)
sentence: ‘we separate ourselves (from impurities)’. This passage
belongs to the halakhic corpus of the Qumran sect.<sup id="fnref:7"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:7')">7</a></sup> 4 QMMT 7 ll.
7-8 contains the following translated text: ‘[ ] We have separated
ourselves from the multitude of the people (<span dir="rtl">העם</span>) and from their
impurities and from intermingling in their practices and in
participating with them in their (practices)’.<sup id="fnref:8"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:8')">8</a></sup> This passage
provides the earliest attested use of the root  <span dir="rtl">פרש</span> in the sense of
withdrawing from the broader community. The term is significant for
identifying the Pharisees and for understanding the designation
 <span dir="rtl">פרושים</span>.<sup id="fnref:9"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:9')">9</a></sup>
* <i>Qal</i> participle  <span dir="rtl">פורס֯</span> in 4Q424 1 l. 2 is interpreted by Clines as
referring to ‘one who separates’.<sup id="fnref:10"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:10')">10</a></sup> This reading however is
uncertain, and alternative interpretations have been suggested.<sup id="fnref:11"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:11')">11</a></sup>
Brin notes that the text is truncated, making it impossible to
determine the precise referent.<sup id="fnref:12"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:12')">12</a></sup>
* In the <i>hiph.</i> stem, forms of  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span> occur seven times, in Leviticus
11:3, 4 (2x), 5, 6, 7, and 26, and are typically translated as ‘that
has hoofs’.<sup id="fnref:13"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:13')">13</a></sup> Parallel occurrences are found in Deuteronomy
14:6–8, where the term appears four times (verses 6, 7 [2x], 8),
likewise meaning ‘that has hoofs’.
*  Additionally, in Psalm 69:32, the masculine participial form
 <span dir="rtl">מַפְִִרִיס</span> is translated as ‘that has horns’.

There are also occurrences of the nominal form  <span dir="rtl">פַּרְסׇה</span>, which
consistently denotes ‘hoof’ or ‘split hoof’.<sup id="fnref:14"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:14')">14</a></sup>


<b>A.2</b>  <i>Akkadian</i>: Scholars frequently draw attention to the isogloss
<i>parāsu</i>, commonly translated as ‘to break [bones]'.<sup id="fnref:15"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:15')">15</a></sup> This verbal
root appears regularly in Akkadian texts with a range of meanings,
including ‘to cut off’, ‘to stop’, ‘to block’, ‘to sever’, ‘to chop
off’, and ‘to dismember’.<sup id="fnref:16"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:16')">16</a></sup> Additionally, it can carry abstract senses
such as ‘to make a decision’ or ‘to investigate’.<sup id="fnref:17"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:17')">17</a></sup> However, this
translation warrants further scrutiny from a linguistic perspective:
<i>parāsu</i> is associated with at least eleven distinct meanings,<sup id="fnref:18"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:18')">18</a></sup> none
of which explicitly conveys the idea of ‘breaking [bones]’. That said,
semantically related meanings such as ‘to chop off’ or ‘to dismember’
are attested and may inform the interpretation.<sup id="fnref:19"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:19')">19</a></sup>


<b>A.3</b>  <i>Arabic</i>: A possible cognate is found in the Arabic verb <i>farasa</i>,
which means ‘to tear’<sup id="fnref:20"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:20')">20</a></sup> or ‘to break the neck’, suggesting a semantic
field associated with forceful division or rupture.<sup id="fnref:21"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:21')">21</a></sup>


<b>A.4</b>  <i>Ugaritic</i>: The feminine noun <i>prst</i> in Ugaritic is of uncertain
meaning, though it has been tentatively interpreted as denoting an
‘offering bowl’.<sup id="fnref:22"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:22')">22</a></sup> It appears, for example, in the expression <i>šmn
prst</i> ‘oil from a bowl’. A potentially related form is the masculine
noun <i>prś / prs</i>, attested as a masculine noun, which refers to a ‘dry
measure’.<sup id="fnref:23"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:23')">23</a></sup> This association may suggest a semantic connection with
the notions of ‘division’ or ‘portion’.<sup id="fnref:24"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:24')">24</a></sup>


<b>A.5</b>  <i>Phoenician, Zincirli inscriptions, official Aramaic</i>: In these
languages, the root <i>prs</i> denotes ‘portion’ and is employed in the
context of measuring commodities such as silver, oil, and grain.<sup id="fnref:25"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:25')">25</a></sup> We
find these meanings of the noun often in the Elephantine papyri.<sup id="fnref:26"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:26')">26</a></sup>


<b>A.6</b>  <i>Palmerene</i>, <i>Mandaen</i>:  <span dir="rtl">פרס</span> means ‘to spread out’.<sup id="fnref:27"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:27')">27</a></sup> Insightful is
the text <i>dkl ‘ḥyd w`l kl prys</i> RIP 145, line 3: “who is all-powerful
and extends himself over everything.”


<b>A.7</b>  <i>Jewish Babylonian Aramaic</i>: 1#  <span dir="rtl">פרס</span> means 'to break, to split'. On the
other hand 2# <span dir="rtl">פרס</span>,  <span dir="rtl">פרש</span> means ‘to spread, distribute’.<sup id="fnref:28"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:28')">28</a></sup>


<b>A.8</b>  <i>Syriac</i>: the root <i>pras</i> conveys the meaning ‘to spread’ or ‘to
stretch out’.<sup id="fnref:29"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:29')">29</a></sup>


<b>A.9</b>  <i>Ethiopic</i>: The verb <i>farasa</i> carries the meaning ‘to be destroyed’
or ‘to be demolished’.<sup id="fnref:30"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:30')">30</a></sup>


<b>A.10</b>  <i>Onomatopeia</i> In a study of the nineteen Hebrew bird names listed in
Book of Leviticus 11:13–19 employing a group-cognition methodology,
Johnson and Jenson investigated possible onomatopoetic correspondences
between the Hebrew bird names and selected bird calls. In summarizing
their findings, they reported that the name  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> most closely
resembled the call of the griffon vulture. A smaller subset of
participants, however, associated  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> with the Egyptian vulture.<sup id="fnref:31"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:31')">31</a></sup>


## 2. Formal Characteristics

<b>A.1</b>
<span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> is a <i>qatl</i> form (BL, 456 jʹ). 



## 3. Syntagmatics

<b>A.1</b> 
In Leviticus 11:13, the  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> occurs within a list of birds (vv
13b-19), clarified by the words  <span dir="rtl">מן העוף</span> (vv 13a). The term  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> appears as the direct object of the Niphal form of the verb  <span dir="rtl">אָכַל</span> (“shall [not] be eaten”) in Leviticus 11:13. The noun is marked by the
definite article and is preceded by the <i>nota accusativi</i>, indicating
its syntactic role as the object of the verb. This construction frames
the list as a set of dietary prohibitions.


<b>A.2</b>  In Deuteronomy 14:12,  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> occurs within a list of birds (vv.
12b–18). The category is clarified by the expression  <span dir="rtl">כָּל־צִפּוֹר</span> in v.
12a, which functions as a variant designation of  <span dir="rtl">מִן־הָעוֹף</span> in Leviticus
11:13a. The list of birds is introduced by a clause containing a Qal
form of  <span dir="rtl">אָכַל</span>—'(and these) you shall not eat (of them)’ (v. 12a). This
construction frames the list as a set of dietary prohibitions.




## <a id="AV"></a>4. Ancient Versions

<b>a. Septuagint (LXX) and other Greek versions (αʹ, σʹ, θʹ)</b>:  

* γρύψ, ‘griffin’: both instances in LXX. 

The Greek term denotes a ‘griffin’.<sup id="fnref:32"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:32')">32</a></sup> In Classical
literature, the term clearly refers to the mythical creature rather than
to an actual bird.<sup id="fnref:33"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:33')">33</a></sup> The griffin is typically described as possessing
the body of a quadruped and the sharply curved beak of a bird of
prey,<sup id="fnref:34"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:34')">34</a></sup> and it is frequently depicted as a guardian of gold (τοὺς
χρυσοφύλακας γρῦπας).

The use of the translation ‘griffin’ may indicate that the natural world
in which the Hebrew Bible was written was unfamiliar to the Greek
translators. Their choice of a creature with the body of a lion and the
head of a vulture may reflect an attempt to preserve the meaning
‘smasher’ for  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span>. One may think, for instance, of the sharp beak of
the vulture and the powerful paws of the lion, both capable of breaking
or tearing. Regarding the use of mythological creatures, Angelini
remarks: ‘This distance is variously expressed through the lens of
mythology or of exoticism, which at this time do not necessarily need to
be considered as two opposite or separate fields’.<sup id="fnref:35"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:35')">35</a></sup>

This usage is noteworthy in light of the translation of  <span dir="rtl">דָאָה</span> in Book of
Leviticus 11:13 as γύψ, the standard Greek term for ‘vulture’.<sup id="fnref:36"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:36')">36</a></sup> In
the parallel passage in Deuteronomy 14:13, the Hebrew text reads
<span dir="rtl">רָאָה</span>
instead of
<span dir="rtl">דָאָה</span>.

-   Other Greek translations (αʹ, σʹ, θʹ)

No variant renderings in other Greek translations are attested.<sup id="fnref:37"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:37')">37</a></sup>

## 5. Lexical/Semantic Fields


<b>A.1</b> 
<span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> belongs to the semantic field of ‘birds/ flyers’.


## 6. Exegesis

### 6.1 Textual Evidence


<b>A.1</b>
Given the semantic flexibility of this root, various interpretations of
the noun have emerged, supported by comparative isoglosses and data from
ancient translations. As demonstrated in Section 2, the verbal root may
yield at least four semantic possibilities: (1) violent breaking, such
as the breaking of bones; (2) non-violent breaking, such as the breaking
of bread; (3) separation, for example, in the sense of separating from
impurities; and (4) division, as in the dividing of hoofs.

The comparative isoglosses suggest the following potential meanings:
(1) violent breaking or destruction (as attested in Arabic, Ethiopic,
and partially in Akkadian);
(2) division (found in Ugaritic, Phoenician, Official Aramaic, and
Zincirli inscriptions);
(3) spreading out or stretching (attested in Palmyrene, Mandaic, and
Syriac).

Ancient translations offer further variation, rendering  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> as either
a type of vulture or as a type of eagle. Modern scholarly discussion
regarding the identity of the bird is informed by this combination of
etymological, comparative, and translational data.


<b>A.2</b>  If the verbal root  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span> is understood in the sense of violent
breaking, this would imply that the noun  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> refers to a bird
associated with such an action. Support for this interpretation can be
drawn from the use of the root in Arabic and Ethiopic, as well as from
Micah 3:3, which references the breaking of bones. Based on this
semantic and contextual evidence, some scholars have identified the
 <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> as the Lammergeier (<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>), a bird of prey known
for its unique behaviour of dropping bones from a height onto rocks in
order to shatter them and access the marrow. <sup id="fnref:41"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:41')">41</a></sup>

Hartley further notes that the Lammergeier feeds on the remains left by
other scavengers, particularly the griffon vulture (<i>Gyps fulvus</i>),
which is often identified with the first bird in the biblical list
(<span dir="rtl">נֶשֶׁר</span>).<sup id="fnref:42"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:42')">42</a></sup> The Lammergeier consumes the marrow from bones it has
broken, thus fitting the profile of a ‘bone-breaker’.<sup id="fnref:43"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:43')">43</a></sup>


Additional support is found in the Akkadian isogloss <i>parāsu</i>, which has
been translated as ‘to break [bones]’. If this connection between the
root  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span> and the act of violent breaking is valid, it raises the
question of whether  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> specifically refers to bone-breaking
behaviour. This interpretation aligns with zoological observations of
the Lammergeier, yet it could also point more broadly to any bird
capable of breaking objects.

For instance, the Egyptian vulture (<i>Neophron percnopterus</i>) exhibits a
similarly remarkable behaviour: it uses stones to crack open eggs. Given
such capabilities, this species too could conceivably be identified with
the  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span>, as both birds demonstrate forms of breaking consistent with
the proposed meaning of the root.<sup id="fnref:44"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:44')">44</a></sup>

[]

Lammergeier (<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>) carrying a bone in its beak
(presumably to drop and break it).

[]

Egyptian vulture (<i>Neophron percnopterus</i>) carrying a stone in its beak
(presumably to break an egg).


<b>A.3</b>  The verbal root  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span> is also interpreted by some scholars as
expressing a non-violent form of ‘breaking’, better rendered as
‘dividing’ or ‘apportioning’. This interpretation stands in contrast to
the violent connotation proposed under section A.1, and there are
several arguments supporting a more moderate semantic range.

First, Micah 3:3 is the only biblical instance often cited to justify
the translation of the Hebrew verbal form  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span> as ‘to break’. However,
this example is uncertain, as the text actually uses the root  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַשׂ</span>,
which must be emended or interpreted as a scribal error for  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span> in
order to support that reading.

Second, although there are several occurrences of  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span> in contexts of
‘breaking bread’ (e.g., Isa 58:7; Jer 16:7; Lam 4:4), these refer to
non-violent, ritualistic, or communal acts. In such cases, ‘breaking’
signifies the act of dividing or sharing, rather than destruction.

Third, while the Akkadian root <i>parāsu</i> is sometimes cited in support of
a violent reading, it never explicitly refers to the breaking of bones
and instead exhibits a wide semantic range—including meanings such as
‘to cut off’, ‘to decide’, or ‘to block’.

Furthermore, although Ethiopic and Arabic data support a more violent
interpretation (e.g., ‘to tear’ or ‘to destroy’), Altmann suggests that
these meanings may represent secondary developments. He posits that the
original sense of the root was ‘to divide’, which then evolved into
‘dividing the spoils of war’, and subsequently came to mean ‘to
destroy’.<sup id="fnref:45"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:45')">45</a></sup> While this is a plausible diachronic explanation, it
remains speculative and requires further evidence; it is currently
unclear how such semantic evolution can be empirically substantiated.

In summary, the first two arguments indicate that the Hebrew and
Akkadian sources offer limited support for translating  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span> as ‘to
break’ in a violent sense. The third argument introduces a theoretical
possibility, though it lacks definitive proof. Moreover, the Ethiopic
and Arabic cognates may represent independent semantic developments
rather than shared meaning. Therefore, there is reasonable linguistic
support for interpreting  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span> as ‘to divide’ rather than ‘to break’ in
a violent sense.

Rather than translating the root as ‘to break’, it may be more accurate
to render it as ‘to divide’ or ‘to separate’. This interpretation is
supported by lexical evidence from Phoenician, the Zincirli
inscriptions, Official Aramaic,<sup id="fnref:46"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:46')">46</a></sup> and possibly Ugaritic.<sup id="fnref:47"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:47')">47</a></sup> Further
support comes from one Qumran text, in which the verb is rendered as ‘to
separate’—a semantic extension of ‘to divide’—specifically in the
context of separation from impure persons or objects.

On the basis of this translation, Altmann proposes that the term may
refer to a bird that travels in a <i>detachment</i> or <i>group</i>, a
characteristic that could apply to migratory birds.<sup id="fnref:48"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:48')">48</a></sup> This opens the
possibility that  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> refers to a non-carnivorous or non-scavenging
species.

However, Altmann also entertains an alternative interpretation in which
the bird is carrion-eating. In this case, the notion of ‘dividing’ would
refer to tearing or removing portions of flesh from a carcass. Such
behaviour could apply to corvids (e.g., crows) or to the Egyptian
vulture (<i>Neophron percnopterus</i>). Notably, Houlihan describes the
Egyptian vulture as ‘the most loathsome of all the scavenging birds’,
noting that it often subsists on human waste and refuse.<sup id="fnref:49"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:49')">49</a></sup>


<b>A.4</b>  In a footnote, Altmann cites a suggestion by Walter Houston,
conveyed through personal communication, in which Houston observes that
<i>qatl</i> forms rarely denote agents.<sup id="fnref:50"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:50')">50</a></sup> Consequently, this implies that
 <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> cannot refer to either the Lammergeier or the Egyptian vulture.
If this interpretation is correct, then none of the birds proposed in
sections A.1 and A.2—namely, egg-breaking or migratory birds—can be
identified as  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span>, since these birds function as agents performing
specific actions.


<b>A.5</b>  The Septuagint renders  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> as γρύψ (‘griffin’). The Greek
translator interprets the  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> as a mythical creature. The vulture
(γύψ) appears as the first bird name in Leviticus 11:14 and Deuteronomy
14:13. Notably, unlike the Hebrew text, the twenty bird names in the
Septuagint are translated, with only the fourth bird in the list
explicitly identified as vulture.


<b>A.6</b>  The translation 'to spread' or 'to stretch out' is attested in both
Classical Hebrew and exegetical literature, though the latter does not
associate the term with any known bird species.


<b>A.6</b>  Finally, the Vulgate follows the Septuagint in rendering the term as
<i>gryps</i> ('griffin'), a mythical creature.


### 6.2  Iconography

<b>A.1</b> Mesopotamian iconographic material frequently depicts both eagles and
vultures, with sufficient detail to distinguish between these two types
of birds.<sup id="fnref:51"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:51')">51</a></sup> However, Bodenheimer notes that while avian
representations are abundant in ancient Mesopotamian art, they are often
highly generalized, conventional, or rendered with a plump form,
rendering the identification of genus—and particularly
species—impracticable.<sup id="fnref:52"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:52')">52</a></sup>

<b>A.2</b> In contrast, Egyptian iconography provides more specific information
regarding birds of prey. Bodenheimer observes that, unlike other
raptors, vultures in Egyptian monumental art can be reliably
differentiated among several species, including the bearded vulture
(<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>), the black vulture (<i>Aegypius monachus</i>), the
griffon vulture (<i>Gyps fulvus</i>), and the Egyptian vulture (<i>Neophron
percnopterus</i>), the latter two being the most commonly represented.<sup id="fnref:53"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:53')">53</a></sup>
The vulture cult constitutes one of the oldest religious traditions in
ancient Egypt.


### 6.3  Zoological information

<b>A.1</b> Based on the information gathered, there is a prevailing tendency to
translate  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> either as a vulture or an eagle. Another possibility is
that it refers to a migratory bird. This section presents the relevant
bird species that are or have been found in Palestine.

If  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> denotes a vulture, five species are or were present in
Palestine. First, the griffon vulture (<i>Gyps fulvus</i>), which was
historically dominant in the region.<sup id="fnref:54"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:54')">54</a></sup> Second, the lammergeier
(<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>), which, according to Tristram, was still commonly
observed in the mountainous areas of Palestine during the nineteenth
century.<sup id="fnref:55"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:55')">55</a></sup> Third, the Eurasian black vulture (<i>Aegypius monachus</i>),
which nests on lower cliff ledges and is often found near human
settlements.<sup id="fnref:56"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:56')">56</a></sup> Fourth, the cinereous vulture (<i>Aegypius monachus</i>;
note: also called cinereous vulture, previously <i>Vultur monachus</i>),
which occurs sparsely throughout the country.<sup id="fnref:57"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:57')">57</a></sup> Fifth, the
lappet-faced vulture (<i>Torgos tracheliotos</i>), currently a rare breeder
in the Negev Desert.<sup id="fnref:58"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:58')">58</a></sup>

Tristram also lists several species of eagles found in
nineteenth-century Palestine, including the golden eagle (<i>Aquila
chrysaetos</i>), the eastern imperial eagle (<i>Aquila heliaca</i>), the lesser
spotted eagle (<i>Aquila pomarina</i>), the greater spotted eagle (<i>Aquila
clanga</i>), the tawny eagle (<i>Aquila rapax</i>), the steppe eagle (<i>Aquila
nipalensis</i>), the short-toed eagle (<i>Circaetus gallicus</i>), the booted
eagle (<i>Aquila pennata</i>), and Bonelli’s eagle (<i>Aquila fasciata</i>).<sup id="fnref:59"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:59')">59</a></sup>
Additionally, Verreaux’s eagle (<i>Aquila verreauxii</i>) should be noted as
present in the area.<sup id="fnref:60"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:60')">60</a></sup>

If  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> is a migratory bird, it could either refer to a large group of
birds or to a specific species. If it functioned as a generic term for
migratory birds, this would imply that a broad range of avian species
was prohibited.<sup id="fnref:61"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:61')">61</a></sup>

Based on the verbal form’s translation, the precise identity of <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> remains indeterminate. However, the ancient versions provide some
guidance, consistently translating the term as a bird of prey. The
variation in these translations suggests that the original meaning of
the word, as well as the Septuagint’s γρύψ (‘griffin’) was followed by
the Latin translations, but also lost early in history. Its placement
among vultures and/or eagles in the biblical lists may have influenced
these divergent translations. The Septuagint’s rendering of γρύψ dates
back to the early Hellenistic period, as the oldest Septuagint
manuscripts of Leviticus originate from this era.<sup id="fnref:62"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:62')">62</a></sup> It is plausible
that there is ancient testimony reflecting an early association of
 <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> with vultures. Conversely, this identification might also stem
from its placement following the  <span dir="rtl">נֶשֶׁר</span>, an animal variously identified
as a vulture or eagle, based on numerous descriptions of its behaviour
and external characteristics within the Hebrew Bible.
 

## 7. Conclusion

<b>A.1</b>
The identification of  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> is constrained by the lack of additional
classical Hebrew texts that describe the animal’s behaviour or physical
characteristics. From the immediate context in Leviticus 11:13-19 and
Deuteronomy 14:11-18, it is clear that <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> refers to a bird. There
are compelling reasons to infer that it is a bird of prey, as the
surrounding bird names in the lists are typically translated as eagles
or vultures. This identification as either a vulture or an eagle is
further supported by ancient versions, which predominantly render the
term as a vulture, and occasionally as an eagle.

The determination of the original meaning largely depends on analyses of
the verbal root  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span>, which appears to describe the bird’s behaviour.
Studies of this root and its cognates in Semitic languages yield two
principal interpretations: first, ‘to break’ in a violent manner, and
second, ‘to spread’ or ‘to divide’. The former interpretation has led to
translations such as ‘breaker’, with proposals identifying  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> as the
lammergeier (<i>Gypaetus barbatus</i>), known for breaking bones, or the
Egyptian vulture (<i>Neophron percnopterus</i>), which breaks eggs using
stones. Despite the appeal of a derivation from ‘violent breaking’, this
interpretation remains uncertain, especially given the usage of the
verbal root  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַשׂ</span> in Micah 3:3, which may represent a scribal variant.
Additionally, cognates with similar meanings appear in Arabic, Ethiopic,
and some Akkadian texts, though the meaning of violent breaking in
Arabic and Ethiopic may represent a secondary semantic development.
Another complication arises from the observation that the <i>qatl</i> verbal
form rarely denotes an agent, which challenges the straightforward
reading of  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> as ‘breaker’. On the other hand, the best translation
of Micah 3:3 is ‘to break’, and the argument that <i>qatl</i> forms cannot
represent agents, is not decisive. Neither is there convincing evidence
that the Ethiopic and Arabic isoglosses are secondary.

The alternative, non-violent meaning of  <span dir="rtl">פָּרַס</span>—that is, ‘to split’ or
‘to divide’—is supported by verbal and nominal forms found in the Hebrew
Bible as well as by most isoglosses. This interpretation opens the
possibility that  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> refers to a migratory bird; however, the exact
identity remains speculative. Another plausible identification is that
of a type of vulture, a view supported by the Septuagint’s translation.

Drawing on the evidence presented, it can be concluded that  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> most
likely denotes a species of vulture or eagle, although a degree of
uncertainty remains. It may refer to a bird that breaks something for
sustenance, consistent with the behaviour of the lammergeier or Egyptian
vulture. Alternatively, it could be a carrion bird, such as the Egyptian
vulture. While the possibility of a migratory bird is not entirely
excluded, but the weight of ancient versions favours the interpretation
of  <span dir="rtl">פֶּרֶס</span> as a form of vulture. Also, the identification as ‘griffin’ in
the Septuagint and the Vulgate supports the interpretation ‘vulture’,
since both birds are characterized by a hooked beak. Accordingly, the
Egyptian vulture and the lammergeier emerge as plausible identification.
