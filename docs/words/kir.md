## 1. <a id="RCM"></a>Root and Comparative Material

<b>A.1</b>  
<i>Semitic?</i>:Gesenius & Roediger, <i>TPC</i>, 672a; <i>BDB</i>, 468b and <i>HAWAT</i>, 176b derive <span dir="rtl">כִּירַיִם</span>, like <span dir="rtl">כּוּר</span>  and <span dir="rtl">כִּיֹּור</span>, from a supposed Semitic root <i>*kwr</i>  ‘to dig’ (cf. Ps. 22:17), and/or ‘to be/make round’ (cf. Orel & Stolbova, <i>HSED</i>, 323, No. 1481). In view of the weak middle radical <i>w/y</i> a direct derivation from <span dir="rtl">כרה</span>  ‘to dig’  and/or *<span dir="rtl">כרר</span>   Pilp. ‘to go round, dance’ is less likely. 

However, <i>GB</i>, <i>KBL</i>, <i>HALAT</i> and <i>HAHAT</i> regard *<span dir="rtl">כִּיר</span> as a loan from Akkadian <i>kīru</i> (see below). This seems a better line 
of derivation. 



<i>Akkadian</i>:
It is difficult to believe that Sumerian gir<sub>4</sub>/kir, Akkadian <i>kīru</i> ‘kiln (for lime and bitumen)’ and Akkadian <i>kūru</i> ‘smelting furnace’ are totally unrelated. Mankowski, <i>ALBH</i> 67-8 deems it possible that the Sumerian word was borrowed from Semitic <i>kūru</i> and that later on Akkadian borrowed it back in the form of <i>kīru</i>. Ellenbogen, <i>FWOT</i>, 83, and Tawil, <i>ALCBH</i>, 160, take the words together. In my opinion it is likely that both words are originally Semitic and derive from a √<i>kwr</i>, cf. Syriac <i>kwr</i> Etpa. ‘to be hot, burn’ and <i>kūrā</i>, ‘fire, furnace’ (Costaz, <i>DSF</i>, 153; Sokoloff, <i>SLB</i>, 612). However, it would seem wise to keep the two nouns apart as denoting different types of heating devices. 




<i>Ugaritic</i>:
<i>kr</i> ‘to be hot, furious, enraged’ (?, cf. Del Olmo Lete & Sanmartín, <i>DULAT</i>, 454).







<i>Postbiblical Hebrew</i>:Jastrow, <i>DTT</i>, 636b: <span dir="rtl">כִירָה</span>, ‘a portable stove on feet, with caves for two pots’; see further Section 6.



<i>Jewish Aramaic</i>:Sokoloff, <i>DJBA</i>, 578: <span dir="rtl">כירי</span> (plural) ‘oven’


<i>Syriac</i>:See above.




<i>Old Arabic</i>:
<i>kwr</i> ‘fire altar’ (Biella, <i>DOSA</i>, 244).



<i>Classical Arabic</i>:
<i>kūr</i> and <i>kīr</i> ‘a blacksmith’s fire-place’ (Lane, <i>AEL</i>, 2637)



<i>Modern Palestinian Arabic</i>:
<i>kûr</i> ‘réchaud en terre cuite’ (stove of baked clay); <i>kûra</i> ‘soufflet de forge’ (blacksmith’s bellows) (<i>DPASyr</i>, 463).



<i>Ethiopic</i>:
<i>kawr</i> ‘furnace’ = Amh. <i>agodo</i> ‘pit in which the blacksmith melts iron’ (Leslau, <i>CDG</i>, 300).

## 6. Exegesis

### 6.1 Textual Evidence


<b>A.1</b> In spite of the masculine form of the dual <span dir="rtl">כִּירַיִם</span>, the singular might well be the feminine <span dir="rtl">כִּירָה</span> which is the normal form in postbiblical Hebrew (cf. <i>GBHrev</i>, § 90e on the plural).
 Lev 11:31b-38 discusses the outcome of all sorts 
of contact (<span dir="rtl">נגע</span>, vv. 31, 36) with the carcass (<span dir="rtl">נְבֵלָה</span>) of dead  reptiles: anything upon which such a creature falls (<span dir="rtl">נפל</span>) will be unclean, any vessel of wood (<span dir="rtl">כְּלִי&thinsp;־עֵץ</span>), garment, skin or sack must be put into water (v. 32). Every earthen 
vessel (<span dir="rtl">כְּלִי&thinsp;־חֶרֶשׂ</span>, cf. Kelso, <i>CVOT</i>, 6-7, No. 5; 18, No. 40) into which it falls shall be broken (<span dir="rtl">שׁבר</span>  Qal), its contents will be unclean (v. 33). After provisions about unclean water and drink in 
v. 34, v. 35 resumes the theme of vv. 32-33.
The general rule of v. 32a is repeated in v. 
35a: everything upon which any part of their 
carcass (<span dir="rtl">מִנִּבְלָתָם</span>) falls shall be unclean. Now it continues with earthenware which is not used as containers of food, but earthenware used to prepare food: oven (→<span dir="rtl">תַֹּנּוּר</span>) and  <span dir="rtl">כִירַיִם</span>. These objects too must be shattered (<span dir="rtl">יֻתָֹּץ</span> <span dir="rtl">וְכִירַיִם</span> <span dir="rtl">תַֹּנּוּר</span>) because they have become unclean and should be treated as such (<span dir="rtl">לָכֶם</span> <span dir="rtl">יִהְיוּ</span> <span dir="rtl">וּטְמֵאִים</span> <span dir="rtl">הֵם</span> <span dir="rtl">טְמֵאִים</span>). Apparently the <span dir="rtl">כִּירַיִם</span> was made of clay, like the oven. It could rather easily be destroyed and, again, remade -- just like the <span dir="rtl">כָּל&thinsp;־כְּלִי&thinsp;־חֶרֶשׂ</span> of v. 33, but in contrast with the <span dir="rtl">כְּלִי&thinsp;־עֵץ</span>  of v. 32, which had to be cleaned in water.

In collocation with the well-known term → <span dir="rtl">תַֹּנּוּר</span> ‘oven’, <span dir="rtl">כִירַיִם</span> may represent some kind of apparatus for baking or cooking. In view of its dual ending it probably had two openings. Nothing is said about its seize.

In the  Qumran Temple 
Scroll, 11Q19, XXXVII.13 <span dir="rtl">כירים</span> occurs in the following context: ‘In the four corners of the courtyard &lt;of the priests&gt; you shall make for 
them a place for the <span dir="rtl">כירים</span>  &lt;14&gt; where they shall cook their sacrifices and the sin-offerings’. As this 
regulation was intended for the four corners, it 
seems most plausible to interprete <span dir="rtl">כירים</span>  here as a plural; but the dual, in 
agreement with Lev. 11:35, is also possible: 
‘make &lt;ldots&gt; a place for a <span dir="rtl">כירים</span>’. 

In rabbinic sources the <span dir="rtl">כירים</span> are mentioned often, e.g, m. Shab., II.1; V.2; m. Kel. 6:2. Usually the word is translated as ‘double stove’ because of its two openings. It was often placed against a wall or in a corner. It was fuelled with stubble or straw. Cf. Krauss, <i>TA</i>, Bd. 1, 90-1; Dalman, <i>AuS</i>, Bd.  4, 15, n. 2; 45, nn. 2-3; 264, n. 2; Bd. 7, 206, n. 3; Brand, <i>KHBH</i>, 230-243.

### 6.3 Archaeology

<b>A.1</b> Small stoves having two openings to receive two cooking pots have been found at various sites (Levine 1989, 205b, n. 20; Milgrom 
1991, 679). Avitzur, 116, Fig. 307 provides a photograph of a small cooking-stove with two openings from Masada (Jacques van Ruiten 
called my attention to this reference, M.D.K.). Another photograph of what is possibly such a double stove is provided by Boling 1975, Fig. 8a.


\includegraphics{double_stove.eps}



Stove for two pots found at Masada (after Avitzur, 116, fig. 307).



The circumstance that the two openings are part of one and the same structure explains the proximity of the apparatus to walls for extra stability.

Kelso takes → <span dir="rtl">אַח</span>, → <span dir="rtl">כִּירַיִם</span> and → <span dir="rtl">תַֹּנּוּר</span> together as ‘the stove family’: ‘All three may be used in holding up a cooking-pot so as to concentrate its heat’ (Kelso, <i>CVOT</i>, 20 [No. 45]).



## 7. Conclusion

<b>A.1</b> Already Gesenius (<i>TPC</i>) gives both possible translations for <span dir="rtl">כִּירַיִם</span>: <i>focus</i>, ‘hearth’, and <i>fornax coquinaria</i>, ‘cooking furnace, stove’ (cf. <i>BDB</i> and <i>DCH</i>). Since in Lev. 11:35 <span dir="rtl">כִּירַיִם</span> is placed next to <span dir="rtl">תַֹּנּוּר</span>  ‘oven’ (for baking), the interpretation ‘stove’ (for cooking) commends 
itself (cf. Baentsch, Jastrow, Kellermann, Cornelius and others; the German 
‘Kochherd’ combines both meanings). Although 
<span dir="rtl">כִּירַיִם</span> is a <i>hapax legomenon</i> in the TeNaKh, whereas →  <span dir="rtl">תַֹּנּוּר</span> is much more frequent, its presence in several Judaic sources including Qumran might indicate that it was a fairly common heating device for the upper classes, among them the priests. After all, a stove with two burners allows for more sophisticated cooking. As it could be smashed into pieces, it must have been made of clay (Dalman).


The rabbinic tradition, including the Targum, and followed by the Peshiṭta, argues in favour of the rendering ‘double cooking-stove’. Archaeological finds seem to confirm the existence of such a cooking device which was placed against a wall or in a corner for greater stability. As a cooking-stove or furnace it is clearly distinguished from its cognate → <span dir="rtl">כּוּר</span>, which has its home in the forge (smelting oven, furnace for metal). There is no evidence for a tripod, as suggested by LXX and Vg.
