Grammatical type: n.f.

## <span id="RCM">1. Root and Comparative Material </span>

<b>A.1</b> <span dir="rtl">שֹׁ֫קֶת</span> is derived from √ <span dir="rtl">שׁקה</span>, ‘to give to drink, to provide water’.<sup id="fnref:1a"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:1a')">1a</a></sup>


<b>A.1</b>: 
<i>Arabic</i> has <i>sāqiya</i>, which may be considered cognate. This word refers to
‘a rivulet, or streamlet, for the irrigation of seed-produce; 
a small channel for the irrigation of 
land’.<sup id="fnref:1"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:1')">1</a></sup> 

<b>A.2</b> 
<i>Aramaic</i>:
Syriac has ܫܩܝܬܐ  (<i>šāqyetā</i>) (feminine emph.), meaning ‘canal’ and ‘female cupbearer’.<sup id="fnref:2"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:2')">2</a></sup> 
The same word is found in plural <span dir="rtl">שקייתא</span> (sg. <span dir="rtl">שקי</span>), ‘trough’
in Tg<small><sup>PsJ</sup></small> Gen 30:38.<sup id="fnref:3"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:3')">3</a></sup> 
Compare the
masculine form ܫܩܝܐ  (<i>šeqyā</i>), 
‘watering’, ‘irrigation’, ‘watercourse’, etc.<sup id="fnref:4"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:4')">4</a></sup>
This is the <span dir="rtl">שָׁקֵי</span>, emph. <span dir="rtl">שָׁקְיָא</span>, 
of Jewish Babylonian Aramaic and Onqelos.<sup id="fnref:5"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:5')">5</a></sup>
This is probably also the same word as the <i>šqy</i> attested in Official 
Aramaic and Palmyrene.<sup id="fnref:6"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:6')">6</a></sup> 
In other forms of Aramaic, a form of the root <i>šqy</i> with preformative
<i>mem</i> is used. Thus in addition to <span dir="rtl">אשקהו</span>,
Samaritan Aramaic has <span dir="rtl">משקי</span>,<sup id="fnref:7"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:7')">7</a></sup> 
which is already attested in Old Aramaic.<sup id="fnref:8"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:8')">8</a></sup>


<b>A.3</b> <i>Post-Biblical Hebrew</i>:
The word <span dir="rtl">שוקת</span> is used in 3Q15 (3QCopper Scroll) 10:16 for a channel, or `water-outlet’.<sup id="fnref:8a"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:8a')">8a</a></sup>
The word also
occurs in halakhic sources concerning
the validness of <i>miqwaʾot</i>: according to mMiq. 4:5 and 5:1, a
trough hewn in the rock --as opposed to one that is fixed to the ground and
should be considered a vessel--, does not make a <i>miqwe</i>
invalid; cf. also mPar. 5:7--6:3 and bSukk. 37a. 
In tPar. 5:9<sup id="fnref:9"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:9')">9</a></sup> 
a trough is described, which is surrounded by
holes or cavities that are linked to the main trough. It is clear from
these instances that the word could refer to different forms of troughs,
constructed in various ways from different materials. In all instances
mentioned, the word <span dir="rtl">שוקת</span> is used for an object that
holds water, without reference to watering livestock.<sup id="fnref:10"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:10')">10</a></sup>

## <span id="Exe">6. Exegesis</span>


<b>A.1</b> 
The meaning of the word <span dir="rtl">שֹׁ֫קֶת</span> is 
‘drinking-trough, watering channel’.<sup id="fnref:24"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:24')">24</a></sup>
Gen 24:20 tells us how Rebekah, Laban’s sister, emptied her jar 
(<span dir="rtl">כַּדָּהּ</span>, → <span dir="rtl">כַּד</span>) into the trough (<span dir="rtl">אֶל־הַשֹׁקֶת</span>) and
ran again to the well (<span dir="rtl">הַבְּאֵר</span>) to draw water for the camels of
Abraham’s servant, who had been sent out to find a wife for Isaac. This is
the only place where a word for ‘drinking-trough’ is found in singular.
Since drinking-troughs are usually referred to in plural, they were often 
found in groups near a well of cistern.<sup id="fnref:25"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:25')">25</a></sup> 
In this case, <span dir="rtl">אֶל־הַשֹׁקֶת</span> should perhaps be interpreted
as ‘into one of the troughs’. The trough in question was positioned at the
top of the stairs which gave access to the source, which was located at the
bottom of a pit (cf. v. 16). The biblical text does not allow conclusions
with regard to the exact form or the material the troughs were made 
of.<sup id="fnref:26"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:26')">26</a></sup>
Dalman has seen troughs made from leather, which can be
taken to the next well; but the usual arrangement, he concedes, is a trough
dug out in the soil, cut out in the rocky bottom, or built from 
stones.<sup id="fnref:27"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:27')">27</a></sup> 
He found them in round and rectangular shapes, but also in the form of a
gutter, up to six meters in length. The latter form would allow more
animals to drink at the same time.

<b>A.2</b> 
The second occurrence of the word <span dir="rtl">שֹׁ֫קֶת</span> may actually refer to the
very same place as where Rebekah met Abraham’s servant. After Jacob had
arranged with Laban that he could take all the spotted and striped animals
from the flocks, he took fresh rods of poplar, almond, and plane, and
peeled white streaks in them. Then in Gen 30:38 it says that he set the
rods that he had peeled ‘in the troughs, in the watering-places, where the
flocks came to drink 
(<span dir="rtl">בָּרֳהָטִים בְּשִׁקֲתוֹת הַמָּיִם אֲשֶׁר תָּבֹאןָ הַצֹּאן לִשְׁתּוֹת</span>) 
in front of the
flocks’. As the animals bred when they came to drink, they did so in front
of the striped rods, which resulted in young that were striped and spotted.
<span dir="rtl">שֹׁ֫קֶת</span> is added here as an explanation of the synonym →
<span dir="rtl">רַהַט</span>, which is probably an Aramaic loanword. --- Another synonym
is <span dir="rtl">מַשְׁאָב</span>.<sup id="fnref:28"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:28')">28</a></sup>







## <span id="Con">7. Conclusions</span>


<b>A.1</b> <span dir="rtl">שֹׁ֫קֶת</span> is a synonym of → <span dir="rtl">רַהַט</span> and 
<span dir="rtl">מַשְׁאָב</span>, and refers to the kind of drinking-troughs that were found near
wells or cisterns, which were constructed in the first place in order to
water livestock. On the basis of modern examples, we may assume that they
were dug out in the soil, cut out in the rocky bottom, or built from
stones. The fact that the Arabic cognate refers to some sort of a channel,
might suggest that a <span dir="rtl">שֹׁ֫קֶת</span> was built in the form of a gutter,
rather than in a round or square shape.
