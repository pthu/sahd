## Introduction <span id="I"></span>

Grammatical type: n.m.  
Occurrences: 2x HB (1/1/0); 0x Sir; 0x Qum.; 0x inscr. (Total: 2). 

* Torah: Num 24:7;
* Nebiim: Isa 40:15.


## 1. Root and Comparative Material <span id="RCM"></span>

<b>A.1</b> <span dir="rtl" lang="he">דְּלִי</span> 
is a derivative (dual) of the verb <span dir="rtl">דלה</span> (I), ‘to draw water’.<sup id="fnref:1"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:1')">1</a></sup>


<b>A.2</b> <span id="Akk"><i>Akkadian</i></span>:  The Akkadian word <i>dālu</i>
is used for a wooden bucket, used especially in irrigation, and also for
the process of irrigation with water drawn from a well.<sup id="fnref:2"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:2')">2</a></sup> 
In a text from Mari
its king claims to have made this form of irrigation obsolete by
the use of canals. It is not clear on what basis the dictionary decided
that the bucket should be made out of wood. It would seem that the
material out of which it was made is undetermined, as
the same word also appears in lists of tribute, where it refers to buckets
made of gold and bronze. Von Soden simply translates 
‘(Schöpf-)eimer’.<sup id="fnref:3"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:3')">3</a></sup>





<b>A.3</b> <i>Aramaic and Post-Biblical Hebrew</i>:
The word <span dir="rtl">דלי</span> is attested a number of times in mosaics in
synagogues.<sup id="fnref:4"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:4')">4</a></sup> 
Hoftijzer and Jongeling translate the word here as ‘bucket’, but explain that in all
instances it is used as an indication of the sign of the Zodiac Aquarius.<sup id="fnref:5"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:5')">5</a></sup>


The Aramaic cognate <span dir="rtl">דול</span> appears not to be attested before Qumran, where it appears in the astrological text 4Q318,
referring to Aquarius.<sup id="fnref:6"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:6')">6</a></sup> 
However, the name <span dir="rtl">דוליאל</span> in a silver amulet
probably from Syria might point to the use of the noun in this 
dialect.<sup id="fnref:7"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:7')">7</a></sup> 
Beyer explains this name as ‘Schöpfeimer Gottes’,<sup id="fnref:8"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:8')">8</a></sup>
but Kotansky, however, would rather see a connection with the pael of the
verb <span dir="rtl">דלי</span>/<span dir="rtl">דלא</span>, meaning ‘to relieve’.<sup id="fnref:9"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:9')">9</a></sup> 
Tal mentions a single occurrence of <span dir="rtl">דול</span>, ‘bucket’, in one of the versions 
of the Samaritan Targum (Tg<small><sup>SmrJ</sup></small> Num 24:7; see <a href="#AV">Ancient Versions</a>).
In Christian Palestinian Aramaic, the status emphaticus ܕܘܠܐ 
(<i>dwl’</i>) is 
attested, but also the forms ܕܠܘ 
(<i>dlw</i>) and ܕܘܠܘ 
(<i>dwlw</i>).<sup id="fnref:10"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:10')">10</a></sup>


Aramaic <span dir="rtl">דול</span> is further attested in Tg<small><sup>J</sup></small>, Tg-II Esth., 
as well as in Jewish Babylonian Aramaic,<sup id="fnref:11"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:11')">11</a></sup> 
in Syriac <span dir="rtl">ܕܘܠܐ</span> (<i>dwl’</i>),<sup id="fnref:12"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:12')">12</a></sup>
as well as Mandaic <i>daula</i>.<sup id="fnref:13"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:13')">13</a></sup> 
About one <i>daula</i> in Mandaic it is said that it came ‘fresh from the kiln’,
which clearly suggests it was made of clay. In none of the forms of Aramaic
is it a foregone conclusion that a bucket should be made out of leather.
Beyer’s ‘(lederner) Schöpfeimer’<sup id="fnref:14"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:14')">14</a></sup> 
seems to be based on the use of the word in Arabic.


In tBer. 4:16<sup id="fnref:15"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:15')">15</a></sup> R. Tarfon is presented with a
<span dir="rtl">דלי של צונין</span>, ‘a bucket of cold water’,
which induces him to discuss the benedictions that one who drinks water to
quench his thirst is supposed to recite. This instance indicates that a
<span dir="rtl">דלי</span> could also be used for drinking-water for human beings. The
same follows from mSukk. 2:5. In GenR. 45.6<sup id="fnref:16"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:16')">16</a></sup> 
the word is used in plural together with <span dir="rtl">בנריות</span>, ‘towels, bathing
apparel’. These are the things that Sarai made Hagar carry for her when she
wanted to take a bath. Thus Sarai humiliated her – an interpretation of the
harsh way in which Sarai treated Hagar according to Gen~16:6. mKel. 14:1
makes clear that a <span dir="rtl">דלי</span> could be made out of metal (a bucket is
mentioned here among <span dir="rtl">כלי מתכות</span>). mShab. 15:2 seems to
indicate that a <span dir="rtl">דלי</span> was usually connected to a rope, but
according to mKel. 14:3 an iron chain was also possible, at least in the
times of the Mishna. yQidd. 1:4, 60b and yB.Bat. 3:1, 13d discuss the issue
of liability if a well that was sold was not covered. Decisive is the
moment when the <span dir="rtl">דלי</span> is handed over. Jastrow  seems to follow
Rashi in interpreting the word in this instance as ‘cover’,<sup id="fnref:17"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:17')">17</a></sup> 
but there is no reason to do so; it refers to the bucket belonging to the 
well;<sup id="fnref:18"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:18')">18</a></sup> 
cf. also bB.Qam. 51b. 


A different use is found in Yal.S. 1.418<sup id="fnref:19"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:19')">19</a></sup> 
and 2.185,<sup id="fnref:20"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:20')">20</a></sup> 
where the word refers to the sign of the Zodiac Aquarius (as on Synagogue
mosaics, cf. above).


Aramaic <span dir="rtl">דולא</span> is not only used for a bucket in Judaic sources,
but also for a trench from which water for irrigation is drawn (bB.Bat. 12a<sup id="fnref:21"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:21')">21</a></sup>) 
as well as for the process of drawing water or irrigation (bB.Meṣ 77a, bM.Qat. 4a, and elsewhere). 

<b>A.4</b> 
<i>Arabic</i>:
The Arabic cognate <i>dalw</i> is indeed used, at least ‘generally’,
for ‘<i>a bucket of leather</i>’.<sup id="fnref:22"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:22')">22</a></sup> 
As in Aramaic, the word may also refer to the sign of the Zodiac Aquarius.



<b>B.1</b> <i>Ugaritic</i>: Koehler and Baumgartner also refer to Ugaritic <i>mdl</i>, with a 
question-mark.<sup id="fnref:23"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:23')">23</a></sup>
This word occurs in KTU 1.3 <span style="text-transform:uppercase;">iv</span> 26 and 1.5 <span style="text-transform:uppercase;">v</span> 7. The
meaning ‘bucket’ seemed to make sense in the latter instance, where the
word is mentioned together with ‘clouds’ and ‘rains’. In the former
instance, however, this translation is awkward, as De Moor has 
pointed out.<sup id="fnref:24"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:24')">24</a></sup>
It is parallel here to <i>qrn</i>,  which, as an attribute of Baal,
probably means ‘ray, flash’. Thus the meaning ‘(thunder)bolt’, which fits
both contexts and is defendable also on etymological grounds, is much more
appropriate.<sup id="fnref:25"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:25')">25</a></sup> 
Ugaritic <i>mdl</i> should therefore no longer be listed among the cognates of 
Hebrew <span dir="rtl">דלי</span>.





<b>B.2</b> <i>Ethiopic</i>: Instead of the cognates mentioned so far, Winton Thomas refers to
Ethiopic <i>dalawa</i>,  ‘to weigh’, and <i>madlot</i>,  ‘weight,
measure, balance, scales, price, value, proportion’,<sup id="fnref:26"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:26')">26</a></sup>
and proposes to vocalize <span dir="rtl">מדלי</span> in Isa 40:15 as <span dir="rtl">מִדְלֶי</span> or <span dir="rtl"> מַדְלֶי</span>, 
translating this part of the verse as ‘Behold, nations are like
the dust of the balances’.<sup id="fnref:27"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:27')">27</a></sup> 
This proposal is unnecessary, and disregards the
corresponding verse 12, where water and dust are also used in parallel
comparisons. 
Moreover, whether or not the Ehiopic <i>dalawa</i> should be
connected with the Hebrew and Aramaic <i>dly</i>,<sup id="fnref:28"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:28')">28</a></sup> 
it is clear that the meaning of ‘weighing’ is restricted to Ethiopic.



## 2. Formal Characteristics <span id="FC"></span>


<span dir="rtl">דְּלִי</span>  is seen as a  <i>qŭṭl</i> formation (dual) of 
√ <span dir="rtl">דלה</span>, ‘to draw water’.<sup id="fnref:29"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:29')">29</a></sup>





## 3. Syntagmatics <span id="Syn"></span>


<b>A.1</b>   <span dir="rtl">דְּלִי</span> is used in a prep. construction with <span dir="rtl">מִן</span>: 


* <span dir="rtl">מִדָּלְיָ֔ו</span>, ‘from his buckets’, Num 24:7; 
* <span dir="rtl">מִדְּלִי</span>, ‘from a bucket’, Isa 40;15.

## 5. Lexical/Semantic Fields <span id="LSF"></span>


<b>A.1</b> The word <span dir="rtl" lang="he">דְּלִי</span> belongs to the semantic field of ‘containers’,<sup id="fnref:45"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:45')">45</a></sup>
and as such a reference to a possible liquid content (<span dir="rtl">מַיִם</span>, ‘water’; 
<span dir="rtl">מַר</span>, ‘drop’) is found in the context. The word is applied in the context of agricultural 
fertile imagery (Num 24:6), or with ‘drop on a bucket’ antithetical to ‘dust (<span dir="rtl">שַׁחַק</span>)
on a balance’ (Isa 40:15).





## 6. Exegesis <span id="Exe"></span>
 


### 6.1 Literal Use <span id="Exe-lit"></span>


<b>A.1</b> Not attested. However, the figurative use 
(<a href="#Exe-fig">→ §6.2</a>), the cognates
(<a href="#RCM">→ §1</a>), some of the versions (<a href="#AV">→ §4</a>), and the Judaic sources 
(<a href="#RCM">→ §1</a>)
point to a literal meaning ‘bucket’.<sup id="fnref:46"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:46')">46</a></sup>
Modern examples, described by 
Dalman, are made of leather, and their mouth is usually
kept open by crossed wooden sticks.<sup id="fnref:47"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:47')">47</a></sup>



### 6.2 Figurative Use <span id="Exe-fig"></span>

<b>A.1</b> After reading Num 24:6, which refers to gardens and ‘cedar trees beside
the waters’, one could be tempted to take the word <span dir="rtl">מַיִם</span> in v. 7a
literally, as if Balaam, whose oracle we are reading here, was predicting
an abundance of water for Jacob’s sowing seed: ‘Water shall flow from his
two buckets, and his seed shall be among much water 
(<span dir="rtl"> ‏יִֽזַּל־מַ֙יִם֙ מִדָּ֣לְיָ֔ו ‏וְזַרְע֖וֹ בְּמַ֣יִם‏ רַבִּ֑ים</span>)’. 
This is, in fact,
the interpretation of most modern commentaries. The gardens and trees
of v. 6, however, are merely images for the prosperity of Jacob’s tents
(v. 5), that is, his family and personnel. In v. 7 Balaam would seem to
introduce a new prophecy in the form of a new image. Gesenius
is probably correct in his explanation of this image:
the water flowing from Jacob’s two buckets (<span dir="rtl">דָּלְיָו</span>;
usually taken as a dual<sup id="fnref:48"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:48')">48</a></sup>) may be
interpreted as a metaphor for <i>semen virile</i>: Jacob will have a
large offspring.<sup id="fnref:49"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:49')">49</a></sup>  
The buckets would then stand for his testicles.<sup id="fnref:50"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:50')">50</a></sup>
As Prov 5:15-18; 9:17; and Cant 4:15 indicate, <span dir="rtl">מַיִם</span> can also
be used as an image for female fertility (or sexual relations in general),
which may be intended in the second part of v. 7a.


Gesenius realized that the image would appear crude to us, and warned the
reader not to be deterred by this. In fact, even the translators of the
Septuagint seem to have thought that a literal rendering would be unclear
or inappropriate, as they give an interpretative one (<a href="#AV">→  §4</a>). But
they understood at least what the text was about. Other proposals include
that of Rouillard, who suggests that the dual refers to the two
buckets of a bucket-chain running over a wheel.<sup id="fnref:51"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:51')">51</a></sup> 
Unfortunately, such machines
appeared in the Middle East only many centuries 
later.<sup id="fnref:52"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:52')">52</a></sup> 
She thinks the image has a double sense: that of ‘abondance luxuriante’ and that of the
new dynasty coming forth from the two kingdoms (Israel and Judah). Though
it is certainly too far fetched to interpret the two buckets as referring
to the two kingdoms, the basis of such an explanation is indeed the
fact that the clause is a figure of fertility and progeny; but Rouillard
does not seem to realize that.<sup id="fnref:53"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:53')">53</a></sup> 
Even more evasive is Levine, who reads,
with many predecessors (cf. already  σ´ in 
<a href="#AV">Ancient Versions</a>), 
‘boughs’ instead
of ‘buckets’ (from <span dir="rtl">דלית</span>, ‘branch’, cf. Ezek 17:6): ‘water drips
from his boughs; his seed grows near plentiful water’.<sup id="fnref:54"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:54')">54</a></sup>


Burrows has independently found the same explanation as Gesenius, but
connects it with an astral motif: the figure in v. 7aA would be that of
Aquarius-Reuben.<sup id="fnref:55"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:55')">55</a></sup> 
This is not convincing, but Burrows’ reference to
Gen 49:4 is helpful, as this verse seems to use water in a comparable
image. Here it is applied in a negative sense, however.<sup id="fnref:56"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:56')">56</a></sup>


<b>A.2</b> In Isa 40:15 the insignificance of the nations before God is likened to a
drop from a bucket 
(<span dir="rtl">הֵ֤ן גּוֹיִם֙ כְּמַ֣ר מִדְּלִ֔י</span>). 
Just as dust, in the parallel clause 
(<span dir="rtl"> וּכְשַׁ֥חַק מֹאזְנַ֖יִם נֶחְשָׁ֑בוּ</span>) 
can hardly be accounted on a scale,
a single drop hardly contributes to the mass of water contained in a full
bucket: this is the position of the nations. God, on the other hand, is
able to measure all the waters in the hollow of his hand, as the
corresponding v. 12 makes clear.

## 7. Conclusions <span id="Con"></span>


A <span dir="rtl">דלי</span> is a bucket, used chiefly to draw water from a well, river or
cistern for purposes of drinking, irrigation, and bathing. In the Middle
East of more recent times, leather was used to make such buckets, but there
is no reason to assume that such a choice of material is implied in the
Hebrew word of biblical times. Even in Mishnaic Hebrew, the word can still
refer to a metal bucket.
