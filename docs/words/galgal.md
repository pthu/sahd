## Introduction <span id="I"></span>



Grammatical type: n.m.   
Occurrences:    13x HB (0/9/4); 1x Sir; 7x Qum; 0x Inscr. (Total: 21).  

* Nebiim: Isa 5:28; 17:13; 28:28; Jer 47:3; Ezek 10:2, 6, 13; 23:24; 26:10;
* Ketubim: Ps 77:19; 83:14; Qoh 12:6; Dan 7:9 (BiblAram);
* Ben Sira: 33:5 (ms F);
* Qum: 1Q32 fr14:2, 3; 4Q186 fr1.i:5; 4Q209 fr6:9; 4Q405 (4QShirShabb<small><sup>f</sup></small>) fr20.ii–21-22:10; 11Q19 (11QTemple<small><sup>a</sup></small>) 34:4, 5, 9;
* Text doubtful: 4Q204 fr1.vii:1; 4Q270 fr6.v:2 (CD x:15); 11Q17 7:12.



## 1. Root and Comparative Material <span id="RCM"></span>


<b>A.1</b>    <span dir="rtl">גַּלגַּל</span> is 
derived from √ <span dir="rtl">גלל</span>, ‘to roll’. 


<b>A.2</b>  <i>Akkadian</i>: 
Mitchell Dahood adduces Akk. <i>gulgullu</i> as support for his proposal to
translate <span dir="rtl">גַּלְגַּל</span> in Qoh 12:6 as ‘water-pitcher’.<sup id="fnref:1"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:1')">1</a></sup>
According to the lexica, however, the meaning
of this word is, in the first place, ‘skull’.<sup id="fnref:2"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:2')">2</a></sup> 
A transferred meaning
is indeed ‘a container shaped like a human skull’, but this sense
occurs only very rarely (once in a list of booty and once apparently
in reference to a cooking pot). Dahood also mentions Punic
<i>glgl</i>, as the word occurs once on a vase (RES 907A). This does not
lend much support to his position either, as the context is too damaged to
conclude that the word actually refers to the object on which it was
written.<sup id="fnref:3"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:3')">3</a></sup> 
Dahood and John Gray, 275, finally propose to consider Ug. <i>gl</i> (KTU 1.14 II 18) 
cognate.<sup id="fnref:4"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:4')">4</a></sup>  
Del Olmo Lete--Sanmartín, 145, do indeed translate this word as ‘copa’ (‘cup’),
but connect it with Hebrew <span dir="rtl">גֻּלָּה</span>, ‘basin’, rather than 
<span dir="rtl">גַּלְגַּל</span>.<sup id="fnref:5"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:5')">5</a></sup>
A better candidate would be the <i>glgl</i> of KTU 1.13.33, for
which the translation ‘cup’ has also been proposed,<sup id="fnref:6"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:6')">6</a></sup> 
but this word should probably be taken
as a verb, meaning ‘to roll’ (<i>gl</i> R), as Johannes de Moor has pointed out.<sup id="fnref:7"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:7')">7</a></sup>

<b>A.3</b> <i>Post-biblical Hebrew</i>:  In 11Q19 (11QTemple<small><sup>a</sup></small>) 34:4-9 and 4Q405 (4QShirShabb<small><sup>f</sup></small>) fr20.ii–21-22:10 it is used
for the wheels of chariots. In CD x:15 <span dir="rtl">גלגל השמש</span> 
indicates the disc or orbit of the sun.<sup id="fnref:8"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:8')">8</a></sup>

In Mishnaic and Amoraic Hebrew, the word <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> can indeed be
used for a wheel that is used to draw water. According to mErub. 10:14, it
is allowed to draw water <span dir="rtl">בגלגל</span> from the Cistern (<span dir="rtl">בור</span>)
of the Exile and the Great Cistern in the Temple on Shabbat, and from the
Haker Well (<span dir="rtl">באר</span>) on a festival. Instead of the <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span>,
which is still used for the two cisterns, tErub. 8:21-22 (ed. Lieberman,
Moʿed, 138) calls the instrument used to draw water from the Haker Well an
<span dir="rtl">השדע</span>, probably ‘scales’, used as a lever. mMid. 5:4
refers only to the Cistern of the Exile and mentions 
<span dir="rtl">הגלגל נתון עליו</span>, 
‘the wheel set over it’. These instances are most
important for us, as there is a strong link in vocabulary: they also
combine the word <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> with the word <span dir="rtl">בור</span>, and there can
be no mistake about the position and function of the <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span>: it is
an object set over a cistern in order to draw water, not a water-pitcher of
some sort. The <span dir="rtl">בית גילגל לרבים</span> of tB.Bath. 2:16
(ed. Lieberman, Neziqin, 137) may be related: it denotes a public well. The
<span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> of ExodR. 31:14 (ed. Mirqin, 6, 72), on the other hand,
clearly describes a wheel with earthenware containers.


The word <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> can also be used for the wheel or wheelwork in
machines, such as in mB.Bath. 4:5, where it is a wheel or screw that forms
part of a press for olives or grapes.<sup id="fnref:9"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:9')">9</a></sup> Elsewhere,
the word refers to the disc or orbit of the sun, celestial sphere and
sphere of the Zodiac, wheel or rotation of fortune, and eye-ball.<sup id="fnref:10"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:10')">10</a></sup> 





<b>A.4</b> 
<i>Jewish Aramaic</i>: 
The Aramaic <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> is used in various meanings in Judaic
sources. In addition to the ‘wheel of buckets’ of LevR. 34:9 and the
pulley of bErub. 104a, which were mentioned above, one other
instance is potentially enlightening for the use of <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> in Qoh
12:6. In LevR. 18:1 (ed. Margulies, 399, following the text of the Oxford
<span style="text-transform:uppercase;">ms</span> given in the apparatus) two Amoraim are discussing the
explanation of the word in this verse: ‘One said: like the clods 
(<span dir="rtl">רגיבייה</span>) 
of Tiberias; the other said: like the <span dir="rtl">גלגלייה</span> of
Sepphoris.’ Unfortunately, we do not know whether the <span dir="rtl">גלגלייה</span> of
Sepphoris are indeed pulley wheels, water wheels, or perhaps boulders (to
close a tomb?).




The word <i>glgl</i> is widely attested in Aramaic in the sense of ‘wheel’
and in various derived meanings. Thus it is a wheel of a cart or chariot in
Old Aramaic (including Samʾalian)<sup id="fnref:11"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:11')">11</a></sup> 
and Biblical Aramaic (Dan 7:6)<sup id="fnref:12"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:12')">12</a></sup>, and the disc of the moon in
an Aramaic text from Qumran (4Q-Astronomical Enoch<small><sup>b</sup></small> (4Q209)
6 l.~6). In Jewish Palestinian Aramaic the word is not only used for the
wheels of a chariot and for a circle or circular form (yPeʿa 5:7, 19a), but
also in the expression <span dir="rtl">גלגלא דאנטילייה</span>, a ‘wheel of
buckets’ (LevR. 34:9, ed. Margulies, 792; cf. RuthR. 5:9, ed. Lerner,
140).<sup id="fnref:13"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:13')">13</a></sup> 
This is probably not a pulley or bucket-chain, but a water-lifting
wheel with compartmented rims,<sup id="fnref:14"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:14')">14</a></sup>
or rather a wheel with earthenware jugs connected to it.<sup id="fnref:15"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:15')">15</a></sup>


In the Aramaic of Tg<small><sup>J</sup></small>, the word is attested in the sense ofwheel, and as ‘rota fortunae’ and ‘die sich kreisende 
Himmelssphäre’.<sup id="fnref:16"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:16')">16</a></sup> 
In addition to these meanings, Jewish Babylonian
Aramaic also knows <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> in the senses ‘eye-ball’, ‘round
object’, and, more important for us, ‘pulley wheel’.<sup id="fnref:17"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:17')">17</a></sup> The latter meaning is found in bErub. 104a, which is
dependent on mErub. 10:14 and refers to a simple pulley (<a href="#Exe">→ §6</a>). For Mandaic <i>gargul</i>, <i>gargla</i>, and various alternative
forms the meanings ‘wheel, convolution,
heavenly sphere, circle’, and ‘rumbling, thunder’ is listed,<sup id="fnref:18"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:18')">18</a></sup> 
but in some instances
the word seems to denote a water wheel or a wheel with compartmented rim.
The Syriac cognate ܓܝܓܠܐ  (<i>gīglā</i>) unites many of the aforementioned 
senses: ‘a wheel, a potter’s wheel, a round stone rolling in a groove to close the opening of a sepulchre, circle, sphere (astronomy), cycle (anual), angels (metaphor alluding to their swiftness), artichoke stalks’.<sup id="fnref:19"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:19')">19</a></sup> 
The use of the word in the sense of
pulley wheel seems rather isolated: it is mentioned only by Robert Payne Smith,
who gives the Peshitta to Qoh 12:6 as the only
attestation.<sup id="fnref:20"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:20')">20</a></sup> 
However, commentaries such as that of John the Solitary indicate that the right sense was
understood and needed no further explanation.<sup id="fnref:21"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:21')">21</a></sup>





<b>A.5</b> 
<i>Latin</i>:
In addition to the Aramaic <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span>, the Latin <i>girgillus</i>
should be mentioned. According to Kutscher this word was used for
a beam serving as a bucket roller (windlass), and would have reached the
Latin language from Phoenician through Canaanite settlements in 
Spain.<sup id="fnref:22"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:22')">22</a></sup> 
This is very well possible, though the development from pulley wheel in Hebrew,
Aramaic, and presumably Phoenician to roller beam in Latin seems slightly
problematic. Kutscher did not see this, as he mistakenly assumed that the
wheel placed over the well functioned as a windlass. I would suggest that
the extension of the sense from pulley wheel to roller beam was an
inner-Latin development, as it appears that the word <i>girgillus</i> was
used in both senses.<sup id="fnref:23"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:23')">23</a></sup>

## 5. Lexical/Semantic Fields <span id="LSF"></span>


<b>A.1</b> 
The word <span dir="rtl">גַּלגַּל</span> ‘wheel’ belongs to two different  semantic fields, namely 
‘parts’ in the domain of ‘objects’,<sup id="fnref:35"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:35')">35</a></sup> 
and ‘wells, cisterns’ in the group ‘artifacts’, domain ‘products’, domain ‘objects’.
In the meaning of ‘whirlwind’ <span dir="rtl">גַּלגַּל</span> belongs to the field of ‘air’, in the group 
‘substances’, domain ‘objects’.<sup id="fnref:36"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:36')">36</a></sup> 
If the word <span dir="rtl">גַּלגַּל</span> is interpreted in the sense of ‘tumbleweed’ in Isa 17:13 and Ps  83:14,
it belongs to the field of ‘plants’ in the group 
‘vegetation’.<sup id="fnref:37"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:37')">37</a></sup>






## 6. Exegesis <span id="Exe"></span>

### 6.1 Literal Use <span id="Exe-lit"></span>

<b>A.1</b> 
In most instances in the Bible, the word refers to a wheel or the wheels of
a cart or a chariot (thus Isa 5:28; 28:28; Jer 47:3; Ezek 10:2, 6, 13;
23:24; 26:10).<sup id="fnref:38"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:38')">38</a></sup> 
However, the <a href="#Exe-fig">figurative use</a>, 
the <a href="#RCM">cognates</a>, 
some of the <a href="#AV">Ancient Versions</a>, 
and the Judaic sources mentioned with the  <a href="#RCM">cognates</a>
indicate that the word could also refer to other kinds of wheels, such as a pulley wheel.





### 6.2 Figurative Use <span id="Exe-fig"></span>

<b>A.1</b> 
Qoh 12:1 tells a young man to remember his Creator in the days of his
youth, ‘before the days of trouble come …’ The following verses continue
to describe the days of trouble and ageing, and conclude by mentioning the
end of life. In verse 6, this is done with the help of four metaphors:
‘Before the silver cord is snapped(?), the golden bowl is broken, the jar
is shattered at the spring, and the <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> falls broken into the
cistern (<span dir="rtl">וְנָרֹץ הַגַּלְגַּל אֶל־הַבּוֹר</span>).’ Verse 7 then
says the same in literal terms ‘(Before) the dust returns to the earth as
it was, and the breath returns to God who gave it.’ It is possible to see
the cistern as a metaphor for the grave (cf. among other instances, Ps
30:4, where <span dir="rtl">בור</span> stands parallel in parallel with the word Sheol,
and Isa 14:19), in which case the <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> would represent the body
or part of it. It is more probable, however, that the image of the broken
<span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> that falls in the cistern expresses, as a whole, something
that does not function any more, as is the case in the other colons of
verse 6.


The obvious translation for <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> is ‘well-wheel’.<sup id="fnref:39"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:39')">39</a></sup> 
We should, however, not think of ‘the wheel that turns the axle of a windlass at a
well’,</b>OED</i>, vol. 20, 111.</i>, 
but of a pulley wheel hanging
over the cistern, used to simplify the task of drawing water. Instead of
grazing the opening of the cistern itself, the rope connected to the bucket
(→§ <span dir="rtl"> דְּלִי</span>) could run over this pulley.<sup id="fnref:40"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:40')">40</a></sup>
Thus the person drawing water could use his or her own body as
counterweight. Pulley wheels like this were known in the Middle East since
the ninth century <span style="text-transform:uppercase;">bce</span>.<sup id="fnref:41"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:41')">41</a></sup>


Other water lifting devices one could think of are the bucket-chain, the
wheel with compartmented rim, or the pot-wheel. These devices appeared at
the beginning of the Hellenistic age,<sup id="fnref:42"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:42')">42</a></sup> 
probably
just in time to reach the author of Qohelet. The bucket-chain consists of a
series of buckets or other containers on a rope or chain, hung over the
cistern or source on a revolving wheel or axle, and was especially popular
in Egypt. It seems less likely, however, that the word <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> would
be used to refer to this contraption as a whole. The pot-wheel or wheel
with compartmented rim are more likely candidates. These wheels were
positioned vertically in a source of water. Earthenware jugs connected to
the wheel or compartments located in the rim are filled with water at the
lowest level and release it when they reach the highest point. The fact,
however, that reference is made to a <span dir="rtl">בור</span>, ‘cistern’ or ‘well’,
makes the pulley wheel the most obvious choice for Qoh 12:6: most cisterns
and wells were simply too deep for wheels with compartmented rim or
pot-wheels.


Nevertheless, Oleson suggests that the <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> of Qoh 12:6 refers to 
a wheel with compartmented rim.<sup id="fnref:43"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:43')">43</a></sup>  
It appears from his book, that he based this idea on the assumption
that a <span dir="rtl">בור</span> can only be a cistern, and that this is ‘a wide, open
tank from which water is usually obtained by dipping from the rim or by
means of run-off spouts.’<sup id="fnref:44"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:44')">44</a></sup> 
This understanding may be influenced by the
meanings of Greek λάκκος, as Oleson is discussing the text of the
Septuagint in this work; it is not fully consonant with the meanings of the
Hebrew word <span dir="rtl">בור</span>. In fact, Oleson himself defines the wheels
(<span dir="rtl">גלגל</span>) set over the cisterns (<span dir="rtl">בור</span>) and well (<span dir="rtl">באר</span>) 
of the Temple as pulley wheels<sup id="fnref:45"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:45')">45</a></sup>
(<a href="#RCM">→ §1</a>), 
and also speaks of the use of pulley wheels for wells and ‘deep
cisterns’ in his later work.<sup id="fnref:46"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:46')">46</a></sup>


An alternative interpretation of the passage as a whole is proposed by
Dahood. He stresses that <span dir="rtl">גַּלגַּל</span> stands in parallel with the
<span dir="rtl">כַּד</span>, ‘jar’, of the first colon of v.~6b, and proposes to
translate the word as ‘water-pitcher’ for that reason.<sup id="fnref:47"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:47')">47</a></sup> 
This argument does
not hold, however, as the parallelism in this verse or the surrounding
verses is not synonymous on a word-for-word basis. Every colon gives a
related but different metaphor for the same idea. The support Dahood and
his follower Gray adduce from Akkadian, Phoenician, and Ugaritic is not
very strong either (<a href="#RCM">→ §1</a>). 
Michael Fox first followed this proposal on the assumption that
the sentences are sequential---together they would describe the
destruction of a well.<sup id="fnref:48"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:48')">48</a></sup> 
Later, however, he admits that four distinct instruments are mentioned, the last of 
which is a wheel.<sup id="fnref:49"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:49')">49</a></sup>
The evidence from Rabbinic Hebrew (<a href="#RCM">→ §1</a>) seems to have played an
important role in this change of opinion.

<b>A.2</b> 
Other figurative uses of the word <span dir="rtl">גלגל</span> are based on transferred
senses of the word derived from the primary meaning ‘wheel’.
Ps 83:14 asks God to make his enemies ‘like a <span dir="rtl">גַּלגַּל</span>, like
chaff (<span dir="rtl">קַשׁ</span>) before the wind’. The word refers here probably to the
wheel-shaped dead stalk of a certain thistle (Gundelia Tournefortii) or
wild Artichoke,<sup id="fnref:50"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:50')">50</a></sup> 
which, blown over the earth by the wind, is rolling like a wheel; 
compare North American Tumbleweed. The same use, this time with
‘chaff on the mountains ({<span dir="rtl">מֹץ הָרִים</span>})’ as its parallel, is found
in Isa 17:13. It has also been proposed to translate the word <span dir="rtl">גַּלגַּל</span> 
in these instances as ‘whirlwind’ or ‘whirling dust’ (see, among
others, <span style="text-transform:uppercase;">nrsv</span>, and cf. Isa 5.28).<sup id="fnref:51"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:51')">51</a></sup> 
This kind of translation would
seem more appropriate in Ps 77:19, ‘the sound of your thunder is in the
<span dir="rtl">גַּלגַּל</span>’, though in this instance one could of course also think
of the roll of thunder.<sup id="fnref:52"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:52')">52</a></sup>


## 7. Conclusions <span id="Con"></span>

<b>A.1</b> 
Biblical Hebrew <span dir="rtl">גַּלְגַּל</span> refers to a round or rolling object; in
the first place a wheel of a cart or chariot, but also, among other things,
a pulley wheel. Placed over a deep well or cistern, such a pulley or
‘well-wheel’ could be used to simplify the task of drawing water: the rope
connected to the bucket ran over this weel, instead of grazing the opening
of the cistern itself. The pulley wheel fallen in the cistern in Qoh 12:6
is a metaphor for the end of life. A rendering ‘water-pitcher’ for this
instance finds no support in the context or in the cognates of this word.
