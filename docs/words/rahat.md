## 1. <span id="RCM">Root and Comparative Material </span>


<b>A.1</b> 
In 1869 Nöldeke still defended the idea
that Hebrew \*<span dir="rtl">רַהַט</span> was a very old loan word from Aramaic: as the
Hebrew equivalent of the Aramaic root <i>rhṭ</i> is
<span dir="rtl">רוץ</span>,  ‘to run’, occurrences of <span dir="rtl">רהט</span> in Hebrew must have
been borrowed from Aramaic.<sup id="fnref:1"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:1')">1</a></sup> 
This <span dir="rtl">רהט</span>, we may add, would then go back to Proto-Semitic <i>rhẓ</i> or 
rather <i>rwẓ</i>, just as Ugaritic <i>r-ẓ</i>,<sup id="fnref:2"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:2')">2</a></sup> 
Akkadian <i>râṣu</i>,<sup id="fnref:3"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:3')">3</a></sup> 
and Ethiopic <i>roṣa</i>.<sup id="fnref:4"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:4')">4</a></sup>
This position is still held by Wagner.<sup id="fnref:5"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:5')">5</a></sup> 


In an 1897 article, however, Nöldeke appears to have changed his position: 
now he connects Hebrew <span dir="rtl">רהט</span> no longer with Syriac 
ܪܗܛ,  ‘to run’,<sup id="fnref:6"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:6')">6</a></sup>
but with Syriac 
ܡܪ̈ܗܛܐ,  ‘goods’,  ‘resources’,<sup id="fnref:7"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:7')">7</a></sup>
and Arabic
<i>rahṭ</i>,<sup id="fnref:8"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:8')">8</a></sup> 
suggesting that this root means  ‘sammeln’ (cf. Arabic <i>rhṭ</i> 
<span style="text-transform:uppercase;">viii</span><sup id="fnref:9"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:9')">9</a></sup>). 
The implication is that in Syriac, two roots are
coming together (i.e., one ending in <i>ẓ</i> and one in
<i>ṭ</i>), as Schulthess pointed out indeed.<sup id="fnref:10"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:10')">10</a></sup>


The assumption of two different roots would also solve the problem that the
obvious cognate for Hebrew <span dir="rtl">רהט</span> in Akkadian is <i>rāṭu</i>
rather than <i>râṣu</i>. 
The former word <i>rāṭu</i> means  ‘channel, runnel’, for irrigation, drainage,
conducting liquids for drinking and libations, as well as for molten
metals, while in transferred meanings, it can refer to the throat or
(birth) canal; in a number of  ‘other occurrences’, however,
 the word is used for a trough.<sup id="fnref:11"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:11')">11</a></sup> 
Koehler & Baumgartner, 
while assuming that
Hebrew <span dir="rtl">רהט</span> goes back to an Aramaic <span dir="rtl">רהט</span> which reflects
Proto-Semitic <i>rwẓ</i>, do mention this <i>rāṭu</i> as a
cognate, but fail to explain how this <i>rwẓ</i> would appear in
Akkadian as <i>rāṭu</i>.<sup id="fnref:12"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:12')">12</a></sup> 
Wagner, on the other hand, was consistent
enough to suppress <i>rāṭu</i> altogether (though he refers to KBL
for  ‘gemeinsemitische Entsprechungen’). 
Other lexica follow the later Nöldeke and distinguish between two roots. 
Consequently, they do not present Hebrew <span dir="rtl">רהט</span>, when used in the sense of
‘drinking-trough’, as a borrowing from Aramaic, but connect it with
Akkadian <i>rāṭu</i> and Arabic 
<i>rhṭ</i>.<sup id="fnref:13"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:13')">13</a></sup>


Though it is indeed easier to defend the distinction between two roots
than the position of KBL, <i>HAL</i>, and Wagner, reality may be a bit more
complicated. The problem is that the two roots cannot be distinguished
completely: the Syriac verb ܪܗܛ  may be used to refer to the
running or flowing of water, precisely what it does in a channel. Should we
speak of a contamination of senses? A good alternative is to assume the
intermediate position also taken by Klein.<sup id="fnref:14"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:14')">14</a></sup> 
The point of departure
would then be a single root <i>rwẓ</i>, which became <i>rhṭ</i>
in Aramaic, and developed different meanings, among which that of  ‘a
channel through which water is running’. 
The word <i>rhṭ</i> in this form and meaning was subsequently 
adopted in Akkadian,<sup id="fnref:15"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:15')">15</a></sup> 
Hebrew, and, with loss of the <i>h</i>, Iraqi Arabic
(<i>rāṭ</i>).<sup id="fnref:16"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:16')">16</a></sup> 
The somewhat difficult
connection with the Arabic root <i>rhṭ</i> could then be dropped.
The only problem that remains is the fact that <i>rhṭ</i> is not
attested in the meaning  ‘channel’ in older Aramaic. 
It does occur, however, in Syriac and 
the Jewish Aramaic of the Targumim (see <a href="#AV">Ancient Versions</a>),
as well as in Mandaic
(<i>r(a)haṭa</i>).<sup id="fnref:17"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:17')">17</a></sup>
The late Hebrew 
use of <span dir="rtl">רהטים</span> in LevR. 31:4<sup id="fnref:18"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:18')">18</a></sup> 
is dependent on Song 7:6 and Gen 30:38.<sup id="fnref:19"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:19')">19</a></sup>
The relation to the Old South Arabian word <i>rhṭn</i> remains
uncertain.<sup id="fnref:20"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:20')">20</a></sup>

## 6. <span id="Exe">Exegesis</span>
 


### 6.1 <span id="Exe-lit">Literal Use</span>


<b>A.1</b> 
The word <span dir="rtl">רַהַט</span> is rendered in the texts of Genesis 30 and Exodus 2 
in its literal sense
‘trough, drinking-trough, gutter’ for watering cattle.<sup id="fnref:34"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:34')">34</a></sup>
After Jacob had arranged with Laban that he could take all the spotted and
striped animals from the flocks, he took fresh rods of poplar, almond, and
plane, and peeled white streaks in them. Then in Gen 30:38 it says
that he set the rods that he had peeled  ‘in the troughs, in
the watering-places, where the flocks came to drink 
(<span dir="rtl">בָּרֳהָטִים בְּשִׁקֲתוֹת הַמָּיִם אֲשֶׁר תָּבֹאןָ הַצֹּאן לִשְׁתּוֹת</span>) 
in front of the flocks’. As the animals bred when they came
to drink, they did so in front of the striped rods, which resulted in young
that were striped and spotted. For this reason v. 41 adds that Jacob put
the rods  ‘in the troughs’ only when the stronger animals were breeding.
Important is the fact that at the first occurrence of the word, the
synonym → <span dir="rtl">שֹׁקֶת</span> is added. If this arrangement goes back
to the author, it could be taken as an indication that the word
<span dir="rtl">רהט</span> was indeed an Aramaic loan word (<a href="#RCM">→ 1</a>), the meaning of
which had to be explained. The use of this loan word would then add to the
‘Aramaic atmosphere’ of the description of Laban’s world (cf. Gen 25:20).


<b>A.2</b> In Exod 2:16, the word is used for the drinking-troughs of the priest of
Midian: though not an Aramaean, at least a foreigner. His
daughters  ‘came to draw water, and filled the troughs to water their
father’s flock’ 
(<span dir="rtl"> וַתָּבֹאנָה וַתִּדְלֶנָה וַתְּמַלֶּאנָה אֶת־הָרְהָטִים לְהַשְׁקוֹת צֹאן אֲבִיהֶן׃</span>). This
sentence suggests that the the troughs were found in the close vicinity of
a well or cistern, from which the water for the flock was obtained.
Since the word occurs in plural only, we should think of some kind of 
an installation, while the context of all instances would indicate a permanent 
arrangement.<sup id="fnref:35"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:35')">35</a></sup> 
The same holds good for
the synonyms → <span dir="rtl">שֹׁקֶת</span> (though singular in Gen 24:20) and
<span dir="rtl">מַשְׁאָב</span>.<sup id="fnref:36"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:36')">36</a></sup> 
The biblical text does not allow conclusions with regard to their
exact form or the material they were made of. Dalman has
seen troughs made from leather, which can be taken to the next well; but
the usual arrangement, he concedes, is a trough dug out in the soil, cut
out in the rocky bottom, or built from stones.<sup id="fnref:37"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:37')">37</a></sup> 
He found them in round and
rectangular shapes, but also in the form of a gutter, up to six meters in
length. The latter form would allow more animals to drink at the same
time.



### 6.2 <span id="Exe-fig">Figurative Use</span>


<b>A.1</b> 
The use of <span dir="rtl">רהט</span> in Song 7:6 has puzzled interpreters from early
times on: Aquila decided just to give a transcription. KBL and Reymond
are among the few modern authorities which hold that this 
<span dir="rtl">רהט</span> is the same as that of Genesis and Exodus, a 
‘drinking-trough’.<sup id="fnref:38"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:38')">38</a></sup>
Neither of them gives an interpretation of the whole phrase, but if one
pays close attention to the structure of the passage Song 7:1-6, this
rendering is not completely impossible. The poet gives a description of the
graceful body of the lady, starting at the feet and moving upward to the
head, ending with the  ‘flowing hair’ that  ‘is like purple’ 
(<span dir="rtl">וְדַלַּת רֹאשֵׁךְ כָּאַרְגָּמָן</span>). 
As no new part of the body is
introduced, Gesenius, followed by others, proposes
<i>cincinni</i>,  ‘curls, locks’, suggesting an etymological link, as
curls are flowing down from the head.<sup id="fnref:39"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:39')">39</a></sup> 
The upward movement is not absolute,
however, as in v.~5 the poet goes from eyes to nose. Therefore one could
argue that in our verse, the poet goes back from the hair to the eyes,
giving the  ‘drinking-troughs’ as a parallel image to the  ‘pools’ of v.~5.


If one considers the fact that by these <span dir="rtl">רְהָטִים</span>  ‘a
king is held captive’ (<span dir="rtl">מֶלֶך אָסוּר בָּרְהָטִים</span>), it is
in fact difficult to think of any other part of the body than the eyes. The
hair is not a good candidate, as a rather unromantic literal interpretation
imposes itself too readily. Other parts of the head, such as the ears,
eyebrows, or mouth, do not make sense either. It is true that Prov  6:25
warns us not to let the adulteress capture us with her eyelashes 
(<span dir="rtl">בְּעַפְעַפֶּיהָ</span>), but these should probably be seen as giving access to the
capturing eye itself. 
skip


<b>B.1</b>  Other solutions are less attractive. 
Thus Gerleman suggests that the word refers to the beam of a loom (cf. Hebrew
<span dir="rtl">רהיט</span>),<sup id="fnref:40"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:40')">40</a></sup> 
and translate  ‘die Fäden deines Haupts sind wie
Königspurpur, an den Bäumen festgemacht.’ This is an inventive
solution, but not a very likely one: hair is supposed to hang down from the
head; it does not look like threads or textile stretched between the beams
of a loom. Rudolph translates <span dir="rtl">רהטים</span> as ‘Lederstreifen’,
but this solution is too complicated.<sup id="fnref:41"><a href="#footnote" data-toggle="modal" onclick="show_modal('fn:41')">41</a></sup>
Rudolph himself already admits that
the poet is describing parts of the body, and not its ornaments. This
forces him to explain this part of the verse as a gloss to account for the
colour purple, of which he thinks that it must have been problematic to the
reader.






## 7. <span id="Con">Conclusions</span>


<b>A.1</b> <span dir="rtl">רַהַט</span> is probably a loan word from Aramaic and is a synonym of
Hebrew →$ <span dir="rtl">שֹׁקֶת</span> and <span dir="rtl">מַשְׁאָב</span>. The word refers to the kind
of drinking-troughs that were made near wells or cisterns in order to
water livestock. On the basis of modern examples, we may assume that they
were dug out in the soil, cut out in the rocky bottom, or built from
stones. Since most of the cognates refer to some sort of a channel, 
a <span dir="rtl">רַהַט</span> may have been built in the form of a gutter, rather
than in a round or square shape.
